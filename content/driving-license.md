# Driving Licence

Denmark drives on the right, has some of Europe's strictest traffic laws, and an excellent public transport system — but if you want to drive, here's everything you need to know about getting a Danish licence as an Indian licence holder.

---

## Indian Licence Validity in Denmark

When you arrive in Denmark with an Indian driving licence, it is **not** automatically valid for long-term use.

### What the Rules Say

- **First 6 months**: You may drive in Denmark on your valid **Indian driving licence** — but only if you are a newly arrived resident
- **After 6 months**: Your Indian licence is **no longer valid** for driving in Denmark as a resident — you must have a Danish licence
- **Tourists**: Tourists from India can drive for up to **90 days** on an Indian licence + **International Driving Permit (IDP)**

> **Important**: Denmark does **not** have a bilateral licence exchange agreement with India. This means you **cannot** simply swap your Indian licence for a Danish one — you must go through the Danish licence process.

### International Driving Permit (IDP)

If you arrive as a tourist or need to drive immediately before getting your Danish licence:
- Obtain an IDP from the **AA India (Automobile Association of India)** or **Motor Vehicles Department** before leaving India
- The IDP is valid for **1 year** and must accompany your original Indian licence
- Available from: WIAA (Western India Automobile Association), AIAC, or regional transport offices
- Cost: approximately ₹500–1,500

## The Danish Driving Licence Process

Since India is not on Denmark's approved exchange list, you must go through the **full Danish licence acquisition process** — including theory and practical tests. The process is split into several phases.

### Overview of the Full Process

```
Phase 1: Enrol in a driving school (kørekortskole)
Phase 2: Complete mandatory theory lessons
Phase 3: Complete mandatory practical lessons
Phase 4: Pass the theory test (teoriprøve)
Phase 5: Complete first aid course (førstehjælpskursus)
Phase 6: Pass the practical driving test (køreprøve)
Phase 7: Receive Danish driving licence (kørekort)
```

---

## Step 1: Enrol in a Driving School

You must enrol in a **Danish driving school (kørekortskole)**. They will guide you through all mandatory modules. You cannot book a test without going through a driving school.

**Finding a driving school:**
- Search on [Kørsels.dk](https://www.korsels.dk) or Google for "kørekortskole" + your city
- Many driving schools in Copenhagen and Aarhus have English-speaking instructors — ask explicitly when enquiring
- Compare prices — there's significant variation

**Estimated full costs** (all-in, Category B car licence):

| Item | Cost (DKK) |
|---|---|
| Driving school enrolment & theory | 3,000 – 6,000 |
| Mandatory practical lessons (minimum hours) | 5,000 – 10,000 |
| Theory test fee (government fee) | 350 |
| Practical test fee (government fee) | 1,050 |
| First aid course | 400 – 800 |
| **Total estimated cost** | **~10,000 – 18,000** |

> **Note**: If you are an experienced driver, you may need fewer practical hours than a complete beginner — instructors can assess this in the first session. Budget for at least 20–30 hours of instruction even with prior experience.

---

## Step 2: Theory Lessons

The theory curriculum in Denmark is structured and mandatory. Topics covered:

- Danish road rules and traffic signs
- Priority rules at intersections
- Motorway driving rules
- Speed limits and safety margins
- Alcohol and drugs — rules and consequences
- Environmental driving (Eco-driving)
- Emergency situations and first response

**Theory resources:**
- **Kørekortbogen** — the official Danish theory book (available in Danish; some schools have English summaries)
- **Kørekort app** — popular Danish app for theory practice questions (has English mode)
- **Driving school theory classes** — usually 7–10 lessons of ~45 minutes each
- **Online theory practice**: [teoriprøven.dk](https://www.teoripRøven.dk) has official practice tests

---

## Step 3: Mandatory Practical Lessons

Before you can book a test, you must complete a minimum set of practical lessons. These are broken into modules:

| Module | Content | Min. Hours |
|---|---|---|
| **Basic driving (grundkøring)** | Gear control, starting, stopping, road awareness | ~10+ |
| **Motorway driving (motorvejskørsels)** | Highway merging, lane changing, overtaking at speed | 1 lesson |
| **Night driving (mørkekørsel)** | Low-visibility driving | 1 lesson |
| **Slippery surface / skid training (glatbanekørsel)** | Controlled skid track, usually at a special facility | Half day |

Your driving school will schedule these for you. The slippery surface course is usually held at a specific test track — it's fun and genuinely useful.

---

## Step 4: The Theory Test (Teoriprøve)

The theory test is a **computer-based multiple choice exam** at an official test centre (Statens Bilinspektion or approved test station).

**Test format:**
- **25 questions** — 4 answer options each
- **3 sections**: General rules (15 questions), Risk recognition/hazard perception (5 questions with images), Technical/environmental (5 questions)
- **Passing score**: Maximum **5 errors** (some sections have stricter limits)
- **Duration**: 25 minutes
- **Language**: Available in **English** — request this when booking
- **Cost**: DKK 350 (government fee, paid via your driving school or directly)

**Booking**: Your driving school typically handles booking once your instructor confirms you're ready.

> **Tip**: Do at least 3–4 full practice tests on [teoriprøven.dk](https://www.teoripRøven.dk) before the real test. The risk-recognition section has unusual picture-based questions — practice these specifically.

---

## Step 5: First Aid Course (Førstehjælpskursus)

Completing a **first aid course** is mandatory before taking your practical test.

- Typically a **4-hour course** covering CPR, choking, accident response, calling emergency services
- Usually offered by your driving school or through organisations like **Falck**, **Røde Kors**, or **TrygFonden**
- Cost: DKK 400–800
- You receive a certificate valid for **5 years**
- Instruction is often available in English — confirm when booking

---

## Step 6: The Practical Test (Køreprøve)

The practical driving test is conducted with a government examiner (**censor**) in your driving school's car.

**Test format:**
- Duration: approximately **45 minutes**
- Route: Standard urban and suburban roads, often including motorway entry/exit
- Evaluation criteria: Safety, road position, observation, speed management, mirror use, decision making

**Common reasons for failing:**
- Incomplete observation at junctions (looking but not turning the head visibly)
- Incorrect speed management on motorways or in residential zones
- Hesitation or being too cautious at roundabouts
- Missing speed limit sign changes

**Cost**: DKK 1,050 (government fee)

If you fail, you can rebook — typically a 2–4 week wait. Each retest costs the same fee.

---

## Step 7: Receiving Your Licence

Once you pass, your driving school submits the results. Your **Danish plastic kørekort** (driving licence card) is issued by the municipality and typically arrives by post within **2–4 weeks**.

- Category B licence is standard (passenger cars, vans up to 3,500 kg)
- **Validity**: 15 years (renewable)
- Issued via your municipality's Borgerservice

## Driving Rules in Denmark

### Speed Limits

| Area | Speed Limit |
|---|---|
| Urban areas (byzone) | 50 km/h (sometimes 40 km/h in residential zones) |
| Open roads (landevej) | 80 km/h |
| Motorways (motorvej) | 130 km/h |
| Near schools, in 30 zones | 30 km/h |

> Speed cameras (**automatisk trafikkontrol**) are widespread and strictly enforced. Fines are significant — and for serious offences, licence suspension is immediate.

### Key Rules of the Road

| Rule | Details |
|---|---|
| **Drink driving** | Blood alcohol limit: 0.5 ‰ (very strictly enforced). For new licence holders in first 3 years: 0.2 ‰ |
| **Mobile phones** | Handheld phone use while driving is illegal — even at red lights. Use hands-free. Fines start at DKK 1,500 |
| **Seat belts** | Mandatory for all passengers at all times |
| **Headlights** | Must be on at all times, day and night (dipped lights / daglygter) |
| **Cyclists priority** | Cyclists have strict legal priority in many situations — give way generously |
| **Roundabouts** | Traffic inside the roundabout has priority (yield on entry) |
| **Emergency vehicles** | Must pull over and yield completely — fines for non-compliance are severe |
| **Winter tyres** | Not legally mandatory but highly recommended Nov–Mar. Some roads require them. |

### Parking Rules

- **Blue zone (blå zone)** — requires a **P-skive** (parking disc), a cardboard clock you set to your arrival time. Free with limited time. Available at supermarkets and petrol stations.
- **Pay & display** — common in city centres. Use the **EasyPark app** (most cities) for cashless parking
- **Yellow kerb lines** — no parking
- **Clearance days (gadefix)** — some residential streets have sign-posted days when cars must be moved for street cleaning. Check signs carefully.

## Buying a Car in Denmark

If you plan to drive regularly, here's a quick guide to buying a car as an expat.

### Registration Tax (Registreringsafgift)

Denmark has one of the highest car purchase taxes in the world — **registration tax** can be **85–150%** of a car's base value. This is why Danish car prices seem so high compared to Germany or the UK.

- A car that costs €20,000 in Germany may cost DKK 350,000–400,000+ in Denmark after registration tax
- Electric vehicles (EVs) have **reduced registration tax** — a major reason EV adoption is very high in Denmark

### Buying a Used Car

The second-hand market is the most practical entry point for most expats.

| Platform | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Den Blå Avis (DBA)** | Denmark's main second-hand marketplace. Huge car section. |
| **Bilbasen.dk** | Car-specific classifieds, large selection |
| **Autouncle.dk** | Price comparison tool — checks if a listed price is fair |
| **Dealer lots** | More expensive but come with warranty and service history |

**Before buying a used car, always:**
1. Check the **DMR (Det centrale Motorregister)** — the official vehicle register — for ownership history, inspection records, and any outstanding debt on the vehicle (vehicles can be repossessed if the previous owner defaulted on a loan)
2. Check [tjekbil.dk](https://www.tjekbil.dk) — free vehicle history report
3. Verify the **syn (vehicle inspection)** is current — cars must pass a roadworthiness test every 2 years
4. Take it for a test drive and ideally have a mechanic inspect it

### Insurance

Car insurance is **mandatory** in Denmark. Minimum cover is third-party liability (**ansvarsforsikring**).

- **Comparison sites**: forsikringsguiden.dk, forsikring.dk
- Popular insurers: **Tryg**, **Topdanmark**, **Alm. Brand**, **Codan**
- Costs vary by car, postcode, and your driving history. Budget DKK 3,000–8,000/year for a standard car.
- Bring your **no-claims history from India** — some Danish insurers will give partial credit (not guaranteed, but worth asking)

### Fuel & Running Costs

| Item | Cost |
|---|---|
| Petrol (per litre) | ~DKK 14–17 |
| Diesel (per litre) | ~DKK 13–16 |
| Road tolls | Denmark has very few tolls — main exception is the Øresund Bridge to Sweden (DKK ~430 one way for a car) |
| Annual road tax (vægtafgift) | Varies by vehicle weight and emissions — DKK 500–5,000/year |
| Vehicle inspection (syn) | DKK 300–500 every 2 years |

## Cycling vs. Driving

Before committing to a car, consider whether you actually need one. In Copenhagen:

- **Cycling is faster** than driving for most urban journeys under 8 km
- Parking in the city is expensive and scarce
- Public transport covers most of the city 24/7
- Car-sharing services like **GoMore**, **Hertz DriveNow**, and **Liftshare** offer flexible alternatives

**When a car makes sense:**
- You live outside the city centre (suburbs, Jutland, rural areas)
- You have children and need to ferry them to activities
- Your workplace is not well-served by public transport
- You want to explore Denmark's countryside and coastline at your own pace
