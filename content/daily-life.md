# Daily Life

The practical stuff that makes Denmark feel like home — or at least survivable in January.

## Indian Grocery Stores

Finding Indian ingredients in Denmark is easier than most people expect — especially in Copenhagen. Here are the main options by city.

### Copenhagen

| Store | Area | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Nørrebro Bazar** | Nørrebrogade, Nørrebro | Large selection of South Asian staples — spices, lentils, rice, fresh produce |
| **Indian Food Store** | Nørrebro | Good for harder-to-find items: curry leaves, hing (asafoetida), Maggi, MTR |
| **Irma (Indian section)** | Various | Stocks Patak's, coconut milk, some spices |
| **Sangam Supermarket** | Frederiksberg | Popular with the Indian community, well-stocked |
| **Aldi / Netto (World Food section)** | Nationwide | Hit-or-miss but sometimes has basmati, lentils, coconut products |
| **Føtex / Bilka** | Nationwide | Larger supermarkets have growing international food sections |

**Online options:**
- **Nemlig.com** — Danish grocery delivery, decent international selection
- **Mad.dk** — delivery from local shops including some ethnic grocers
- **Amazon.de** — for packaged goods, ships to Denmark (in EUR)

### Aarhus

- **Basaren** (Grønnegade area) — main international food market
- **Salling** / **Storcenter Nord** for mainstream supermarkets with international sections
- Various Middle Eastern/Pakistani grocery shops in the city centre often stock Indian staples

### Odense

- **Bazaar Fyn** — large international food market with Indian ingredients
- Several smaller Asian/Middle Eastern shops in the city centre

> **Tip**: Curry leaves, drumstick, fresh methi, and some regional Indian vegetables are harder to find. Many families order these via Indian community WhatsApp groups that organise bulk buys from Hamburg or Amsterdam.

## Healthcare System

Denmark has a universal, tax-funded healthcare system. As a CPR-holder, you have access to the full system — essentially free at the point of use.

### Registering with a GP (Læge)

Your **first step** for healthcare is registering with a **General Practitioner (GP)**, known as a **praktiserende læge** or just **læge**.

1. Go to [sundhed.dk](https://www.sundhed.dk)
2. Log in with MitID
3. Search for GPs accepting new patients in your postcode area
4. Register — you'll receive a **sygesikringsbevis** (health insurance card, now fully digital)

**Important**: You must register with a GP in your own municipality. Copenhagen has a GP shortage — act quickly after getting your CPR. Some GPs have waiting lists.

### How the System Works

- **GP first** — for all non-emergency healthcare, you see your GP first. They refer you to specialists as needed.
- **Specialists (specialists)** — referred by your GP, free of charge
- **Emergency (Skadestue / Akutmodtagelse)** — A&E for genuine emergencies. Not for flu or minor ailments.
- **1813** — Emergency medical helpline (phone, not 112). Call for medical advice or non-life-threatening urgent issues.
- **112** — Ambulance / life-threatening emergencies only

### Dental Care

**Dental care is not covered** by the public system for adults. You pay out-of-pocket or via private insurance. Most employers provide supplemental private health insurance that covers dental — check your contract.

Average costs without insurance:
- Checkup + cleaning: DKK 800–1,500
- Filling: DKK 700–1,200

### Prescriptions

Medicines are partially subsidised through a **Medicintilskud** scheme. The amount subsidised depends on annual spending — the more you spend, the higher the subsidy percentage. Use **sundhed.dk** to track your subsidy.

### Mental Health

Mental health support is available through your GP (referral to **psykolog** or psychiatrist). Private therapy is more common for expats — typical cost DKK 800–1,400/session. Many private therapists in Copenhagen work in English.

## Seasonal & Weather Tips

Denmark's climate is a significant adjustment for anyone from India. Here's what to expect and how to prepare.

### The Four Seasons

| Season | Months | Conditions | What to Expect |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Winter** | Nov – Feb | 0–5°C, dark, often rainy/grey | Sunset at 3:45 PM in December. Very little sunlight. |
| **Spring** | Mar – May | 5–15°C, more light, some rain | Daylight rapidly increases, quite pleasant |
| **Summer** | Jun – Aug | 18–25°C, long days | Midnight dusk in June. Beautiful and social. |
| **Autumn** | Sep – Oct | 10–18°C, leaves, rain | Cozy season, but weather deteriorates by October |

### Surviving Winter

The hardest part of Danish life for most Indian expats is the **dark winter**. Copenhagen gets only about 7 hours of daylight in December, and overcast skies reduce that further.

**Practical tips:**
- **Vitamin D supplements** — nearly every expat in Denmark takes these from October to April
- **Light therapy lamp (dagslys lampe)** — widely used, genuinely effective for mood. Buy one at Jysk, IKEA, or online
- **Get outside daily** — even 20 minutes of outdoor light during lunch helps significantly
- **Layer properly** — the Danish concept of **hygge** involves being warm and cosy indoors. Invest in good base layers and a quality winter jacket
- **Invest in waterproof boots** — rain and slush are constant October–March. Proper rubber-soled waterproof footwear is not optional

**Winter gear checklist:**
- Thermal base layers (Decathlon Novadry range is affordable and excellent)
- Quality down or wool-insulated jacket (-10°C rated for comfort in 0°C weather)
- Waterproof outer shell layer
- Gloves, scarf, hat — all essential by November
- Waterproof boots or warm ankle boots

### Seasonal Affective Disorder (SAD)

SAD (also called winter depression) affects many people in Scandinavia, including expats. Symptoms include low mood, fatigue, and appetite changes during dark months. It's normal, common, and treatable. If you struggle significantly, speak to your GP — light therapy, physical activity, and sometimes medication are all options.

### Summer in Denmark

Danish summer (June–August) is genuinely beautiful and makes up for the winter. Daylight lasts until 10–11 PM. Cafes and restaurants spill outdoors. The entire country seems to cheer up simultaneously.

- Take all your holiday leave in summer if possible
- Book popular summer rentals early — Danes book summer cottages (sommerhus) months in advance
- Embrace the outdoor culture — parks, beaches, cycling

## Transportation

### Cycling

Denmark is one of the world's most cycle-friendly countries. In Copenhagen, **cycling is faster than driving** for most urban journeys.

- Buy a decent second-hand bike within your first month (DKK 800–2,500 for a good used city bike from FB Marketplace or [Den Blå Avis](https://www.dba.dk))
- Lights are **legally required** at night — police do fine for this
- Bike lanes (cykelsti) are everywhere and generally respected

### Public Transport

- **Rejsekort** — a rechargeable contactless travel card for all public transport (Metro, S-tog, buses, regional trains). Buy at any Metro station or DSB website.
- The **Copenhagen Metro** is excellent — runs 24/7, clean, reliable
- **S-tog** — suburban train network covering greater Copenhagen
- **DSB** — national rail for inter-city travel (Copenhagen–Aarhus: ~3 hours)
- **Rejseplanen app** — essential navigation app for all public transport in Denmark

### Driving

- Indian driving licences are valid for a period after arrival, but you must exchange it for a Danish licence eventually
- Denmark drives on the right
- Speed cameras are common — do not speed
- Electric vehicles are very common; charging infrastructure is good
