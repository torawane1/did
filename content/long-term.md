# Long Term

If you've decided Denmark is home — or you're considering making it so — here's a practical guide to the path from work permit holder to permanent resident and eventually citizen, with coverage of family visas and schooling.

---

## Permanent Residency (Permanent Opholdstilladelse)

### The Standard Path

Under the standard route, you can apply for permanent residency after **4 years** of continuous legal residence in Denmark, provided you meet a set of conditions.

**Core requirements (standard route):**

- [ ] **4 years of continuous legal residence** in Denmark
- [ ] **Self-support**: Must not have received certain social welfare benefits in the 3 years before application
- [ ] **Employment**: Must be in ordinary employment or self-employment (or have been for most of the period)
- [ ] **Danish language**: Pass **Prøve i Dansk 2** (PD2) test — Danish language Level B1
- [ ] **Citizenship test**: Pass an **Active Citizenship test**
- [ ] **Clean criminal record**: No convictions above certain thresholds
- [ ] **No outstanding debt to the state**

### Fast-track Path (3 Years)

A **3-year fast-track** to PR exists if you meet **additional conditions** on top of the above:

- Hold a relevant STEM or other listed degree
- Have been **continuously employed** in a qualifying role
- Meet a higher salary threshold (check current figures at SIRI — roughly DKK 500K+ per year)
- Language: Pass **Prøve i Dansk 3** (PD3) — Danish Level B2

### Maintaining Your Permit While Working Towards PR

- Keep your annual **work permit renewal** up to date — gaps in coverage can reset the clock
- Do **not** leave Denmark for more than **6 months in a row** without a re-entry permit (udrejsebevis) — this may be considered abandonment of residence
- Check if your permit allows extended travel — business travel is usually fine, but consult SIRI if unsure

## The Danish Language Requirement

The language requirement is the piece most people underestimate. You need to pass a formal test — simply "getting by at work in English" does not count.

### Danish Language Courses

- **Danskuddannelse** is free for immigrants — enrol via your municipality (Kommunen)
- Three levels available: Danskuddannelse 1, 2, and 3 (DU3 is for academic-level learners — most tech professionals are enrolled here)
- Classes are typically 2–3 evenings per week
- **Time commitment**: Plan for 2–3 years of consistent study to reach B2 (PD3)

### Practical Tips

- Start language classes **as soon as you arrive** — the earlier you start, the shorter the path to PR
- Many companies subsidise or pay for Danish classes as a benefit — ask HR
- Apps like **Babbel**, **Duolingo**, and **Clozemaster** help supplement formal classes
- Watch Danish TV (DR1, TV2) — Netflix has subtitles in Danish; turn them on for shows you know
- The **Ordbogen** app is the best Danish-English dictionary

### The Tests

| Test | Level | When Needed |
|---|---|---|
| **Prøve i Dansk 2 (PD2)** | B1 | Standard PR track |
| **Prøve i Dansk 3 (PD3)** | B2 | Fast-track PR (3 years) and citizenship |
| **Studieprøven** | C1 | Higher education equivalent — not required for PR |

Tests are administered by municipalities. Booking in advance is essential — slots fill up months out.

## Family Reunification (Family Dependent Visas)

Once your own residence permit is approved, your family can join you.

### Spouse / Partner

**For a married spouse:**

- Apply via SIRI ([nyidanmark.dk](https://www.nyidanmark.dk)) — the application is joint with you (the reference person)
- Requirements are tied to **you**, not your spouse:
  - You must have a valid residence permit with the right to bring family
  - Your accommodation must be of sufficient size (at least 20 m² per person)
  - You must be able to self-support (not received certain benefits)
  - You must have resided in Denmark for at least 2 years (typically)
  - **Attachment requirement**: Denmark calculates an "attachment score" — you must have a stronger attachment to Denmark than to any other country. For recent arrivals this can be complex — a lawyer helps.

**Spouse's rights once approved:**
- Full right to work in Denmark (no separate work permit needed)
- Access to Danish language courses
- Can apply for their own permanent residency after meeting residency requirements

**Unmarried partners**: Denmark recognises cohabiting partnerships — but you must demonstrate the relationship is genuine and of a certain duration. Consult an immigration lawyer.

### Dependent Children

- Children under 15 generally have an easier path — fewer conditions apply
- Children 15–18: more requirements apply
- Children must apply before turning 18 for family reunification

### Processing Times

Family reunification applications typically take **3–6 months** from submission. SIRI timelines vary — apply early and track via the SIRI online case portal.

## Citizenship (Statsborgerskab)

Danish citizenship is one of the most valued in the world — and one of the more rigorous to obtain.

### Eligibility Timeline

Under the current rules (2026), you can typically apply for citizenship after:

- **9 years** of continuous legal residence in Denmark (standard route)
- **8 years** if you pass the **Prøve i Dansk 3 (PD3)** language test at B2 level or higher
- **7 years** under certain exceptional circumstances

> These timelines can change — the Danish Parliament periodically revises citizenship legislation. Verify the current requirements at [nyidanmark.dk/statsborgerskab](https://www.nyidanmark.dk).

### Requirements for Citizenship

- Sufficient years of residence (above)
- Danish language: Pass **Prøve i Dansk 3 (PD3)** — B2 level
- Pass the **Statsborgerskabsprøven** (citizenship test) — 40 questions on Danish society, history, and culture
- Self-support: Not received certain social benefits for a specified period
- Clean criminal record
- Renounce other citizenships — **India does not allow dual citizenship**. This is a major, irreversible decision.

### India Does Not Allow Dual Citizenship

This is the defining factor for most Indian nationals. Becoming a Danish citizen means **surrendering your Indian passport**.

**What you lose:**
- Indian citizenship and the right to stay in India without a visa
- Indian property ownership restrictions may apply (NRIs can own property in India, but PIOs / foreign nationals have more restrictions for agricultural land)
- Access to Indian government services tied to citizenship

**What you may gain:**
- Visa-free access to ~185 countries (Danish passport)
- Full EU freedom of movement
- Ability to vote in Danish elections
- Enhanced job security and no permit renewal stress

**OCI Card (Overseas Citizen of India):**
After surrendering Indian citizenship, you can apply for an **OCI (Overseas Citizen of India)** card. OCI holders:
- Can visit India visa-free indefinitely
- Can work, study, and bank in India
- Cannot vote in Indian elections or hold government office
- Cannot own agricultural land in India

**Most Indian expats who naturalise in Denmark apply for OCI immediately after surrendering their passport.** It is not automatic — apply at the Indian Embassy in Copenhagen after naturalisation.

## Schooling for Children

If you have children, here's a practical overview of the Danish education system.

### Ages and Levels

| Stage | Ages | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Vuggestue** (nursery) | 0–3 | Heavily subsidised; register early (long waitlists) |
| **Børnehave** (kindergarten) | 3–6 | Mandatory from age 5 (børnehaveklasse) |
| **Folkeskole** (primary + lower secondary) | 6–16 | Years 1–9, free, taught in Danish |
| **Gymnasium / STX / HF** (upper secondary) | 16–19 | Equivalent to A-levels |
| **University** | 18+ | Free for EU/EEA residents; tuition fees apply for non-EU/EEA |

### Practical Considerations

**Language of instruction**: The public Folkeskole teaches primarily in Danish. Children pick up Danish quickly (typically fluent within 1–2 years), but the first year can be challenging for older children.

**International schools**: Copenhagen and Aarhus have several international schools teaching the IB (International Baccalaureate) or national curricula:

| School | Location | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Copenhagen International School (CIS)** | Hellerup | Largest international school, IB curriculum |
| **Rygaards International School** | Hellerup | Ages 5–16, English |
| **Virum School of Languages** | Virum | Strong international programme |
| **QSI International School** | Copenhagen | American curriculum |

International schools charge tuition (DKK 60,000–130,000/year). Some employers provide an education allowance for senior hires — negotiate this.

**Recommendation for young children (under 10)**: Public Folkeskole is often recommended — Danish children are extremely welcoming and children learn Danish fast in an immersive environment. Integration is smoother at younger ages.

### Childcare Costs and Subsidies

Public childcare is heavily subsidised:
- **Vuggestue/Børnehave**: Typically DKK 1,500–2,500/month (means-tested subsidy available — you get more subsidy at lower incomes)
- Private Daycare: Can be DKK 4,000–8,000/month, but subsidised as well
- Apply for childcare through your municipality — **join the waitlist as early as possible**, ideally before you even arrive

## Planning Ahead: A Long-Term Roadmap

| Year | Milestone |
|---|---|
| Year 1 | Arrive, settle, CPR, work permit active |
| Year 1–2 | Start Danish language classes; ensure family reunification in motion |
| Year 2–3 | Pass PD2 (B1) Danish test |
| Year 3 | Eligible for PR if on fast-track (with PD3 pass + salary conditions) |
| Year 4 | Eligible for PR on standard track (with PD2 pass) |
| Year 5–6 | Pass PD3 (B2) Danish test if not already done |
| Year 7–9 | Eligible to apply for citizenship (depending on test results and legislative changes) |
