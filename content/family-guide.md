# Family Guide

Moving to Denmark with a family is a very different experience from moving alone. Denmark is one of the best countries in the world to raise children — but navigating the visa process, finding schools, and settling in takes planning. This guide covers everything from bringing your family over to choosing the right school.

---

## Bringing Your Family to Denmark

### Who Qualifies as a Dependent?

Under Danish immigration law, you can bring the following family members to Denmark:

- **Spouse or registered partner** — legally married or in a registered partnership
- **Cohabiting partner** — if you have lived together for at least 18 months (cohabitation partner visa)
- **Unmarried children under 18** — biological or legally adopted children

> **Note**: Parents, siblings, and extended family members cannot come as dependents under standard family reunification rules.

### Family Reunification (Familiesammenføring)

Family reunification is the formal visa category for bringing a spouse and children to Denmark. It applies whether you are on a work permit or permanent residency.

**Requirements (for the sponsor — i.e., you, in Denmark):**
- Valid residence permit with at least 3 months remaining
- Sufficient income (the "attachment requirement" — you must earn above a minimum threshold, which changes annually — check [nyidanmark.dk](https://www.nyidanmark.dk) for the current figure)
- Adequate housing (minimum floor space requirements apply — roughly 20 m² per additional person)
- No outstanding debt to the Danish state
- No serious criminal record

**Requirements for your family member:**
- Valid passport
- Proof of relationship (marriage certificate, birth certificates)
- No record of serious crime

### Application Process

1. **You (the sponsor) apply on behalf of your family** at [nyidanmark.dk](https://www.nyidanmark.dk/en-GB/You-want-to-apply/Family/Family-reunification)
2. Your family members may also need to attend a **biometric appointment** at the Danish Embassy in India (New Delhi or Mumbai)
3. **Processing time**: typically **3–6 months** — apply as early as possible
4. Upon approval, your family receives a **residence permit card** valid for the same duration as your own permit

**Fees (2026 approx.):**
| Applicant | Fee (DKK) |
|---|---|
| Spouse / partner | ~3,410 |
| Child | ~1,990 |

### Spouses: Right to Work

Once your spouse receives their family reunification permit, they have **full and unrestricted right to work in Denmark** — no separate work permit is needed. They can:
- Work in any field, for any employer
- Start their own business
- Study at Danish universities or language schools

> This is one of Denmark's most family-friendly immigration features — your spouse does not need to find a job offer before arriving.

### Children's Residence Permit

Children under 18 receive a residence permit tied to the family. Once they turn 18, they need to apply for their own residence permit — usually as a student or via another qualifying route.

## Healthcare for Families

Once your family members have their CPR numbers, they are entitled to the **full Danish public healthcare system** at no cost — including:

- **GP registration** — each family member registers with their own GP via [sundhed.dk](https://www.sundhed.dk)
- **Paediatric care (børnelæge)** — children's health referrals through the GP
- **Dental care for children under 18** — **free** through the municipal children's dental service (tandpleje). This is a significant benefit compared to adult dental costs.
- **Maternity and postnatal care** — fully covered, including midwife visits, hospital birth, and postnatal check-ups
- **Vaccinations** — the Danish childhood vaccination programme is free and comprehensive

## Childcare in Denmark

Denmark has world-class publicly subsidised childcare. Wait lists can be long — **register as early as possible**, ideally before or on the day your child is born / arrives.

### Types of Childcare

| Type | Age Range | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Vuggestue** (nursery) | 6 months – 2 years | Municipal nurseries, subsidised |
| **Dagpleje** (childminder) | 6 months – 2–3 years | Home-based childcare by a licensed carer, cheaper alternative |
| **Børnehave** (kindergarten) | 3 – 6 years | Pre-school, most children attend |
| **SFO / Fritidshjem** | 6 – 10 years | After-school care attached to primary schools |

### Costs & Subsidies

Denmark subsidises childcare heavily — you pay a **maximum of 25% of the actual cost** (means-tested; lower income = less you pay). The municipality pays the rest.

**Approximate parent contribution (Copenhagen, 2026):**
| Childcare Type | Parent Cost (DKK/month) |
|---|---|
| Vuggestue (full time) | ~3,500 – 4,500 |
| Børnehave (full time) | ~2,000 – 3,000 |
| SFO | ~1,200 – 2,000 |

> Siblings receive a **discount** on the second child. Families on lower incomes may pay significantly less through the income-based subsidy scheme.

### How to Apply for a Childcare Place

1. Contact your **municipality's childcare department (Pladsanvisningen)** as soon as you arrive
2. Register on the **municipal waiting list** — online portals vary by municipality (e.g., Copenhagen uses [kk.dk](https://www.kk.dk))
3. Waiting times vary: **2–12 months** is common in Copenhagen. In smaller cities, shorter.
4. If you need care before your place comes through, ask about **emergency places** (nødpasning)

> **Tip**: You have a legal right to a childcare place within your municipality within **3 months** of applying (children between 26 weeks and school age). However this is not always easy to enforce quickly in practice.

## Schools in Denmark

Denmark has two main options for school-age children: **Danish public schools** and **international/private schools**.

### The Danish Public School System (Folkeskole)

The **folkeskole** is Denmark's free public school system, covering ages **6–16** (Year 0 to Year 9, with an optional Year 10).

**Key features:**
- Completely **free** — no tuition fees
- Instruction is entirely in **Danish**
- Small class sizes (typically 20–28 students)
- Strong focus on group work, critical thinking, and wellbeing — not just academic performance
- No homework until older years — Denmark's educational philosophy prioritises play and balance
- **SFO** (after-school care) available at most schools until ~5 PM

**For Indian families**, the main consideration is **language**. Children who arrive speaking no Danish are given **reception classes (modtagelsesklasse)** — special integration classes that run for 1–2 years before transitioning into mainstream Danish classes. Most children pick up Danish surprisingly quickly, especially those under 10.

> **Reality check**: Children from India adapt well to the Danish school system. The environment is less pressured than Indian schooling — no rote memorisation, no board exams. Parents often find this a positive change; some find it unsettling. Both reactions are normal.

## International Schools in Denmark

If you prefer English-medium education — or if your stay in Denmark may be temporary — international schools are the main alternative.

### Copenhagen

| School | Curriculum | Ages | Annual Fees (DKK) | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Copenhagen International School (CIS)** | IB (PYP, MYP, DP) | 3 – 18 | ~130,000 – 155,000 | Largest, most prestigious. Campus in Nordhavn. |
| **Rygaards International School** | British (IGCSE, A-Level) | 3 – 18 | ~100,000 – 130,000 | Long-established, smaller community |
| **Bernadotte School** | Danish + International | 5 – 16 | ~65,000 – 85,000 | Bilingual (Danish/English), more affordable |
| **Hellerup International School** | IB PYP | 3 – 11 | ~90,000 – 110,000 | Primary years only |
| **QSI International School** | US curriculum | 3 – 18 | ~100,000 – 130,000 | American system |
| **ISAK (International School Aarhus)** | IB | 6 – 18 | ~80,000 – 110,000 | Main option in Aarhus |

> **IB (International Baccalaureate)** is the most widely recognised international curriculum in Denmark and globally. The **IB Diploma** (ages 16–18) is accepted for university admission in India, the UK, the US, and across Europe.

### Employer-Sponsored School Fees

Many multinational employers in Denmark — especially those hiring expats — will **partially or fully cover international school fees** as part of the relocation package. This is worth negotiating explicitly in your contract, as school fees can be a significant expense.

### Aarhus

- **International School Aarhus (ISAK)** — IB curriculum, well-regarded, main option for the second city

### Odense & Smaller Cities

International school options are limited outside Copenhagen and Aarhus. Families in smaller cities generally use the Danish folkeskole (with modtagelsesklasse support) or commute to Aarhus for school.

## After-School Activities for Children

Denmark has an excellent network of children's activities, all heavily subsidised:

| Activity | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Music school (musikskole)** | Municipal music schools offer instrument lessons from age 6. Very affordable. |
| **Sports clubs (idrætsforeninger)** | Football, swimming, gymnastics, martial arts — all community-run, cheap |
| **Gymnastics (gymnastik)** | Enormous in Denmark. Most towns have a community gymnastics club. |
| **Scouting (spejder)** | Danish scouting is very active, outdoor-focused, and social |
| **Art and creative workshops** | Many kulturhuse and libraries run children's workshops |
| **Swimming lessons** | Mandatory in the folkeskole, but many families also do private lessons |

Search [DGI's club finder](https://www.dgi.dk) for activities near you.

## Housing Considerations for Families

### Space Requirements

Danish apartments are generally smaller than what Indian families with children might expect. When looking for family housing:

- A **3-bedroom apartment** (75–100 m²) is the minimum comfortable size for a family of four
- Most family apartments in Copenhagen are in suburbs or surrounding municipalities — **Frederiksberg, Gentofte, Lyngby, Gladsaxe, Ishøj** are popular with families
- **Detached or semi-detached houses** are common in suburban and provincial areas
- Look for proximity to schools, childcare, and public transport

### Family-Friendly Areas in Copenhagen

| Area | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Frederiksberg** | Dense, urban, excellent schools, very popular with expats |
| **Gentofte / Hellerup** | Affluent northern suburbs, CIS and Rygaards schools nearby |
| **Lyngby-Taarbæk** | More suburban, good schools, DTU nearby |
| **Valby** | More affordable, up-and-coming, good transport links |
| **Vanløse** | Family-friendly, parks, calmer than the city centre |
| **Ishøj / Brøndby** | More affordable, large Indian and South Asian community |

## Financial Benefits for Families

Denmark provides substantial financial support for families with children.

### Børne- og Ungeydelse (Child Benefit)

**All children resident in Denmark under age 18** are entitled to child benefit from the Danish state — regardless of nationality. This is paid automatically once your child has a CPR number.

**Rates (2026 approx., per child per quarter):**
| Age | Quarterly Amount (DKK) |
|---|---|
| 0 – 2 years | ~5,500 |
| 3 – 6 years | ~4,400 |
| 7 – 14 years | ~3,450 |
| 15 – 17 years | ~2,900 |

> These are paid automatically by **Udbetaling Danmark** once your child is registered. No application is usually needed.

### Maternity & Paternity Leave

Denmark has one of the world's most generous parental leave systems.

- **Total leave**: up to **52 weeks** per couple
- **Mother**: minimum 4 weeks before birth + 14 weeks after (barsel)
- **Father/co-parent**: 2 weeks immediately after birth (fædreorlov), plus additional shared weeks
- **Pay**: up to DKK ~4,460/week from the state (dagpenge), plus most employers top this up to full salary — check your contract
- Since 2022 reforms, **24 weeks are now earmarked per parent** (to encourage equal sharing), with 9 flexible weeks

> If you are on a work permit, you are entitled to parental leave if you have been working and paying into the system. Confirm your specific entitlement with your employer and HR.
