# Family Guide

Moving to Denmark with a family is a very different experience from moving alone. Denmark is one of the best countries in the world to raise children — but navigating the visa process, finding schools, and settling in takes planning. This guide covers everything from bringing your family over to choosing the right school.

---

## Bringing Your Family to Denmark

### Who Qualifies as a Dependent?

Under Danish immigration law, you can bring the following family members to Denmark:

- **Spouse or registered partner** — legally married or in a registered partnership
- **Cohabiting partner** — if you have lived together for at least 18 months (cohabitation partner visa)
- **Unmarried children under 18** — biological or legally adopted children

> **Note**: Parents, siblings, and extended family members cannot come as dependents under standard family reunification rules.

### Family Reunification (Familiesammenføring)

Family reunification is the formal visa category for bringing a spouse and children to Denmark. It applies whether you are on a work permit or permanent residency.

**Requirements (for the sponsor — i.e., you, in Denmark):**
- Valid residence permit with at least 3 months remaining
- Sufficient income (the "attachment requirement" — you must earn above a minimum threshold, which changes annually — check [nyidanmark.dk](https://www.nyidanmark.dk) for the current figure)
- Adequate housing (minimum floor space requirements apply — roughly 20 m² per additional person)
- No outstanding debt to the Danish state
- No serious criminal record

**Requirements for your family member:**
- Valid passport
- Proof of relationship (marriage certificate, birth certificates)
- No record of serious crime

### Application Process

1. **You (the sponsor) apply on behalf of your family** at [nyidanmark.dk](https://www.nyidanmark.dk/en-GB/You-want-to-apply/Family/Family-reunification)
2. Your family members may also need to attend a **biometric appointment** at the Danish Embassy in India (New Delhi or Mumbai)
3. **Processing time**: typically **3–6 months** — apply as early as possible
4. Upon approval, your family receives a **residence permit card** valid for the same duration as your own permit

**Fees (2026 approx.):**
| Applicant | Fee (DKK) |
|---|---|
| Spouse / partner | ~3,410 |
| Child | ~1,990 |

### Spouses: Right to Work

Once your spouse receives their family reunification permit, they have **full and unrestricted right to work in Denmark** — no separate work permit is needed. They can:
- Work in any field, for any employer
- Start their own business
- Study at Danish universities or language schools

> This is one of Denmark's most family-friendly immigration features — your spouse does not need to find a job offer before arriving.

### Children's Residence Permit

Children under 18 receive a residence permit tied to the family. Once they turn 18, they need to apply for their own residence permit — usually as a student or via another qualifying route.

## Healthcare for Families

Once your family members have their CPR numbers, they are entitled to the **full Danish public healthcare system** at no cost — including:

- **GP registration** — each family member registers with their own GP via [sundhed.dk](https://www.sundhed.dk)
- **Paediatric care (børnelæge)** — children's health referrals through the GP
- **Dental care for children under 18** — **free** through the municipal children's dental service (tandpleje). This is a significant benefit compared to adult dental costs.
- **Maternity and postnatal care** — fully covered, including midwife visits, hospital birth, and postnatal check-ups
- **Vaccinations** — the Danish childhood vaccination programme is free and comprehensive

## Daycare & Childcare in Denmark

Denmark has one of the world's best publicly subsidised childcare systems. For families with small children, understanding how Danish daycare works — from registration to daily routines — is essential. This section covers everything in detail.

> **Critical first step**: Register your child for a daycare place **as early as possible** — ideally on the day your child is born or the day you arrive in Denmark. Waiting lists are real, especially in Copenhagen.

### Understanding the Danish Childcare System

Denmark's childcare philosophy is fundamentally different from India's. There is no academic pressure on young children — the focus is entirely on **play, socialisation, emotional development, and outdoor time**. Danish daycare is not "pre-school" in the Indian sense. Children do not learn to read, write, or do maths in daycare. Instead, they learn to cooperate, express themselves, resolve conflicts, and become independent.

This can be a culture shock for Indian parents who may expect structured academic learning from a young age. Trust the system — Denmark consistently ranks among the top countries globally for child wellbeing, and the pedagogical approach is backed by extensive research.

### Types of Childcare

Denmark offers several types of publicly subsidised childcare, each suited to different age groups and family needs:

| Type | Danish Name | Age Range | Setting | Typical Group Size |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Nursery** | Vuggestue | 6 months – 2 years | Institutional (purpose-built facility) | 10–14 children per group |
| **Childminder** | Dagpleje | 6 months – 2–3 years | Private home of a licensed carer | 3–4 children per minder |
| **Kindergarten** | Børnehave | 3 – 6 years | Institutional (often attached to or near schools) | 20–25 children per group |
| **Integrated institution** | Integreret institution | 0 – 6 years | Combined nursery + kindergarten in one facility | Varies |
| **After-school care** | SFO / Fritidshjem | 6 – 10 years | Attached to primary schools | 20–30 children |

#### Vuggestue (Nursery) — In Detail

The vuggestue is the most common institutional daycare option for infants and toddlers. Key details:

- **Staff-to-child ratio**: approximately **1 adult to 3–4 children** (one of the best ratios in Europe)
- **Opening hours**: typically **6:30 AM – 5:00 PM** (some until 5:30 PM), Monday to Friday
- **Staffing**: run by trained **pædagoger** (pedagogues — equivalent to early childhood educators with a 3.5-year university degree) and **pædagogmedhjælpere** (assistants)
- Children are assigned to a **stue** (group room) with dedicated staff who stay with the same group, providing continuity
- Each child has a dedicated **kontaktpædagog** (primary pedagogue) — your main point of contact for your child's development

#### Dagpleje (Childminder) — In Detail

Dagpleje is a home-based alternative where a licensed municipal childminder cares for **up to 4 children** in their own home. Key details:

- **More intimate setting** — ideal for very young babies or children who may find larger group settings overwhelming
- Childminders are **trained and supervised** by the municipality, with regular inspections
- **Smaller groups** provide more one-on-one attention
- Often cheaper than vuggestue
- Childminders typically have a **gæstedagpleje** (guest childminder) arrangement — if your minder is sick, your child goes to a designated backup carer
- Some dagplejere (childminders) meet up in groups during the week, so your child still gets group socialisation
- **Limitation**: less structured than institutional daycare; the quality depends heavily on the individual minder

#### Børnehave (Kindergarten) — In Detail

At age 3, children transition from vuggestue/dagpleje to børnehave. This is effectively universal — **98% of Danish children aged 3–5 attend børnehave**.

- **Focus**: social skills, creative play, nature exploration, early language development (in Danish)
- **Staff-to-child ratio**: approximately **1 adult to 6–7 children**
- **Mandatory pre-school year (børnehaveklasse)**: at age 5–6, children enter a mandatory preparatory year (Year 0) that bridges kindergarten and folkeskole. This is attached to the school, not the børnehave.
- Many børnehaver have **forest kindergarten days** (skovbørnehave) where children spend entire days outdoors in nature, rain or shine

#### Integreret Institution (Integrated Institution)

Many municipalities now operate integrated institutions that combine vuggestue and børnehave under one roof. Benefits:

- **No transition disruption** — your child stays in the same institution from 6 months to 6 years
- Older and younger children interact, which benefits both age groups
- Simpler for parents with multiple children of different ages
- Increasingly the norm, especially in newer facilities

### The Settling-In Period (Indkøring)

When your child starts daycare for the first time, there is a structured **settling-in period** lasting **1–3 weeks**. This is standard across Denmark and taken very seriously.

**Typical indkøring schedule:**

| Day(s) | Duration | Parent Present? |
|---|---|---|
| Day 1–2 | 1–2 hours | Yes, stay with your child the entire time |
| Day 3–4 | 2–3 hours | Stay briefly, then leave for 30–60 minutes |
| Day 5–7 | 3–4 hours | Drop off, leave, return after a few hours |
| Week 2 | Half-day (4–5 hours) | Drop off and pick up only |
| Week 3 | Full day | Normal routine |

> **Important for Indian parents**: The indkøring period is not optional — your daycare will expect **at least one parent** to be available for the full settling-in period. Plan your work schedule accordingly. Most Danish employers understand this and will accommodate it.

### A Typical Day in Danish Daycare

Understanding what your child's day looks like helps manage expectations:

| Time | Activity |
|---|---|
| 6:30 – 8:30 | Arrival and free play (children arrive at different times) |
| 8:30 – 9:00 | **Morgenmad** (breakfast) or morning fruit/snack |
| 9:00 – 9:30 | **Samling** (circle time) — songs, stories, calendar, weather |
| 9:30 – 11:00 | **Outdoor play** — in the playground, nearby park, or forest |
| 11:00 – 11:30 | **Frokost** (lunch) — hot meal provided by the institution |
| 11:30 – 13:30 | **Middagslur** (nap time) for younger children — often in **prams outdoors** |
| 13:30 – 14:00 | Wake up, quiet play |
| 14:00 – 14:30 | **Eftermiddagsmad** (afternoon snack) |
| 14:30 – 17:00 | Free play, creative activities, outdoor time until pick-up |

> **Outdoor napping**: One of the most striking aspects of Danish daycare for Indian families — babies and toddlers nap **outside in their prams**, even in winter (down to about -10°C). This is deeply embedded in Danish culture and backed by research showing children sleep longer and more soundly outdoors. Your child will be warmly bundled in a sleeping bag. It is completely safe and normal.

### Food & Meals in Daycare

Most Danish daycare institutions provide **all meals** (breakfast, lunch, and afternoon snack) as part of the daycare fee. This is a significant benefit.

**What to expect:**
- **Lunch** is typically a **hot cooked meal** — simple, nutritious Danish food (pasta, meatballs, soup, fish, potatoes, vegetables)
- **Breakfast and snacks** are usually bread (rugbrød — rye bread), fruit, vegetables, and dairy
- Meals are **organic** — Danish law requires at least 90% organic food in municipal daycare
- Milk and water are the standard drinks; no juice or sugary drinks

**Important considerations for Indian families:**

- **Meat is standard**: pork, chicken, and beef are all commonly served. If your family is vegetarian or has dietary restrictions, **inform the daycare in writing** during registration. Most institutions will accommodate vegetarian diets, but you may need to provide specific alternatives.
- **No spicy food**: Danish daycare food is very mild by Indian standards.
- **Pork**: This is commonly served. If your family does not eat pork (Hindu, Muslim, or other reasons), clearly communicate this. Institutions are accustomed to handling this — Denmark has a diverse population and most daycare centres already manage multiple dietary requirements.
- **Allergies**: taken very seriously. Document all allergies during intake, and the institution will manage them rigorously.

### Costs & Subsidies

Denmark subsidises childcare heavily. By law, parents pay a **maximum of 25% of the actual operating cost**. The remaining 75% (or more) is paid by the municipality through tax funding.

**Approximate parent contribution (Copenhagen, 2026):**

| Childcare Type | Parent Cost (DKK/month) | Approximate EUR/month |
|---|---|---|
| Dagpleje (childminder, full time) | ~3,100 – 3,700 | ~415 – 500 |
| Vuggestue (nursery, full time) | ~3,500 – 4,500 | ~470 – 600 |
| Børnehave (kindergarten, full time) | ~2,000 – 3,000 | ~270 – 400 |
| SFO (after-school, full time) | ~1,200 – 2,000 | ~160 – 270 |

**Key financial details:**

- **Sibling discount (søskenderabat)**: if you have more than one child in municipal daycare, you pay **full price for the most expensive place** and receive a **50% discount** on all additional children
- **Income-based subsidy (fripladstilskud)**: families with lower household income may qualify for **reduced fees or completely free childcare**. The subsidy is calculated based on your combined household income. Apply through your municipality.
- **Part-time options**: some municipalities offer part-time places (e.g., 30 hours/week instead of full-time) at a reduced rate — useful if one parent works part-time or is on parental leave
- Fees are paid **monthly** and are usually auto-deducted via PBS (the Danish automatic payment system)

> **Comparison with India**: Danish daycare is expensive by Indian standards but heavily subsidised compared to private childcare in most Western countries. The quality of care — trained staff, organic food, low ratios — is exceptional. Many Indian families are positively surprised by the value they receive.

### How to Apply for a Childcare Place

#### Step-by-Step Registration

1. **Get your child's CPR number** — this is required for registration. Apply at your local Borgerservice as soon as possible after arrival.
2. **Register on the municipal waiting list (venteliste)** — this is done through your municipality's online portal:
   - **Copenhagen**: [kk.dk/pladsanvisning](https://www.kk.dk) (Digital Pladsanvisning)
   - **Aarhus**: [aarhus.dk](https://www.aarhus.dk) (search for "dagtilbud")
   - **Odense**: [odense.dk](https://www.odense.dk) (search for "pasning")
   - Other municipalities: search "[municipality name] pladsanvisning"
3. **List your preferred institutions** — you can typically list **3–5 preferences** ranked by priority
4. **Specify your desired start date** — this should be as early as possible, ideally the day your child turns 26 weeks (6 months)
5. **Wait for an offer** — the municipality will contact you with an available place. You usually have **48 hours to 2 weeks** to accept or decline.

#### Waiting Times

| City | Typical Wait Time | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Copenhagen (central) | 3–12 months | Longest waits, especially for vuggestue |
| Copenhagen (suburbs) | 2–6 months | Shorter in outer areas like Brønshøj, Vanløse |
| Aarhus | 2–6 months | Varies by district |
| Odense | 1–4 months | Generally shorter |
| Smaller towns | 1–3 months | Often quicker, sometimes immediate |

#### Your Legal Right: The Childcare Guarantee (Pasningsgaranti)

Danish law guarantees every child aged **26 weeks to school age** a childcare place within their municipality. Key details:

- Your municipality must offer a place **within 3 months** of your application (or from the desired start date, whichever is later)
- The place may not be at your **preferred** institution — you may be offered a place elsewhere in the municipality
- If the municipality cannot honour the guarantee, they must provide a **private alternative at no additional cost to you**
- If you are offered a place and decline it, you go back to the bottom of the waiting list

> **Tip**: If you're not offered a place within 3 months, contact your municipality's Pladsanvisning directly and reference the **pasningsgaranti**. This legal right is well-established, and municipalities generally comply.

### Choosing Between Daycare Options

Indian families often wonder which type of daycare is best. Here is a practical comparison:

| Factor | Vuggestue (Nursery) | Dagpleje (Childminder) |
|---|---|---|
| **Group size** | 10–14 children per group | 3–4 children |
| **Setting** | Purpose-built institution | Childminder's private home |
| **Staff** | Multiple trained pædagoger | One primary carer |
| **Socialisation** | More peer interaction | More intimate, family-like |
| **Outdoor facilities** | Larger playgrounds, more equipment | Smaller, varies by home |
| **Stability** | Always open (staff rotate if sick) | Backup arrangements needed if minder is sick |
| **Cost** | Slightly higher | Slightly lower |
| **Best for** | Social, outgoing children; parents who want structure | Shy or sensitive children; very young infants |

> **Recommendation**: For Indian families arriving with very young children (under 1 year), dagpleje can be a gentler introduction — smaller group, more personal attention, and a home-like environment that may feel more familiar. For children aged 1–2 who are more social, vuggestue offers richer stimulation and peer interaction.

### Private Daycare Options

In addition to municipal daycare, Denmark has several private alternatives:

#### Private Institutions (Privatinstitutioner)

- Privately run but **publicly subsidised** — parents pay the same as municipal daycare (the municipality pays the subsidy directly to the private institution)
- Must meet the same quality standards as municipal institutions
- Some have **specific pedagogical approaches** (Waldorf, Montessori, nature-focused, bilingual)
- May have **shorter waiting lists** than popular municipal institutions
- Search for private institutions on your municipality's website or on [tilbudsportalen.dk](https://tilbudsportalen.dk)

#### Private Childminding (Privat Pasning)

- You can hire a **private nanny or au pair**, or use a private childminding scheme
- The municipality provides a **private childminding subsidy (privat pasning tilskud)** — you receive a direct payment from the municipality towards the cost (typically 75% of the dagpleje rate)
- **Requirements**: the carer must be approved by the municipality, and the arrangement must meet minimum standards
- Useful if you cannot get a municipal place, want a carer who speaks your language, or prefer home-based care on your own terms

#### International / Bilingual Daycare

A few daycare institutions in Copenhagen offer **English-language or bilingual programmes**:

- These are rare and often have **long waiting lists**
- They typically cost more than standard municipal daycare (as they operate as private institutions)
- Examples include international kindergartens attached to international schools (e.g., CIS Early Years at Copenhagen International School)
- **Consider carefully**: immersing your child in Danish-language daycare is one of the fastest ways for them to learn Danish, which will benefit them enormously in school and social life

### Language Considerations for Indian Families

One of the biggest concerns for Indian parents is the **language barrier**. Your child will be in an entirely Danish-speaking environment.

**Key points to understand:**

- **Children learn languages remarkably fast**: a child placed in Danish daycare at 1–2 years old will typically be **conversational in Danish within 3–6 months**. Children under 3 absorb languages almost effortlessly.
- **Older toddlers (2–3 years)** may take slightly longer and may experience a temporary "silent period" where they understand but don't speak. This is normal and passes.
- **Staff will communicate with you in English** or use translation tools during drop-off, pick-up, and parent meetings. Most pædagoger speak functional English.
- **Parent communication** (daily updates, newsletters, event invitations) is usually in Danish only. Use Google Translate or DeepL — or ask a Danish-speaking friend to help.
- **AULA** is the digital communication platform used by most daycare institutions and schools. All messages, photos, and event notifications come through AULA. Download the app immediately.
- **Your child will become bilingual**: speaking Danish at daycare and your home language at home. This is a significant advantage. Maintain your home language actively — read to them, speak to them in Hindi/Marathi/Tamil/etc. Research strongly supports bilingual upbringing.

> **Personal tip for Indian parents**: Do not worry about your child "losing" your home language. Children who grow up bilingual from a young age maintain both languages. What they may initially lose is the ability to discuss certain topics (like what happened at daycare) in your home language, because they learned those experiences in Danish. This is normal and temporary.

### What to Bring / Prepare for Daycare

Your daycare will give you a specific list, but here is a general guide:

**Essential items to bring:**

- **Extra clothing** — at least 2 full changes of clothes (including socks and underwear), kept in your child's cubby at all times
- **Rain gear (regntøj)** — waterproof jacket and trousers. This is **essential** — Danish children play outside in all weather. Buy from brands like Reima, CeLaVi, or Mikk-Line.
- **Rubber boots (gummistøvler)** — for muddy/rainy days
- **Winter gear** — warm snowsuit (flyverdragt), mittens, hat, wool underlayers. Danish winters require serious gear (November–March).
- **Indoor shoes (hjemmesko)** — soft-soled slippers for inside use
- **Sunscreen and sun hat** — for summer months. Some institutions ask parents to apply sunscreen before drop-off.
- **Nappies/diapers (bleer)** — some institutions provide them, others ask parents to supply their own. Clarify during intake.
- **A comfort item** — a favourite stuffed animal or blanket for nap time (sutteklud, bamse)
- **A family photo** — many institutions display family photos so children can see their families during the day

> **Label everything** with your child's name. Danish institutions have many children and identical-looking rain gear. Use iron-on name labels or a permanent marker.

### Sick Child Policies (Sygdomspolitik)

Danish daycare has strict policies about sick children. Understanding these prevents awkward situations:

- **48-hour rule**: your child must be **symptom-free for 48 hours** before returning after vomiting or diarrhoea
- **Fever**: children with a fever (over 38°C) must stay home. If your child develops a fever during the day, you will be called to **pick them up within 1 hour**
- **Conjunctivitis (pink eye)**: children must stay home until symptoms clear (usually 2–3 days with treatment)
- **Hand, foot, and mouth disease**: common in daycare. Children can return once they feel well and have no fever, even if spots remain.
- **Head lice (lus)**: extremely common in Danish daycare. Not a cause for alarm — treat with lice shampoo and fine-comb. The institution will notify all parents if lice are detected.
- **Antibiotics**: if your child is prescribed antibiotics, most institutions expect the child to stay home for the **first 24 hours** of treatment

> **Important**: Danish daycare will call you if your child is unwell. Ensure both parents' phone numbers are registered, and that at least one parent can leave work to pick up the child. Danish employers generally understand this — taking a sick day to care for a child (barnets første sygedag) is a legal right under most Danish collective agreements.

### Holiday Closures & Reduced Hours

Danish daycare institutions close on **all Danish public holidays**. In addition:

- **Summer closure**: most institutions close for **3 weeks in July** (weeks 28–30 typically). During this period, the municipality usually offers a combined "summer daycare" at a designated institution — you can sign up your child for this alternative care.
- **Christmas/New Year**: closed from **24 December to 1 January** (or nearby — exact dates vary by municipality)
- **Bridge days (lukkedage)**: institutions may close on certain bridge days (e.g., the Friday after Ascension Thursday). These are announced at the beginning of the year.
- **Reduced hours on planning days (pædagogiske dage)**: 1–3 days per year where staff have planning meetings and the institution either closes early or closes entirely

> **Plan for closures**: as an Indian family, you likely don't have extended family nearby to help during closures. Plan ahead — coordinate with other families for shared childminding, or arrange annual leave to cover closure periods.

### Parent Involvement & Communication

Danish daycare expects a moderate level of parent involvement:

- **Forældremøde (parent meetings)**: held 1–2 times per year. Attending is expected. Conducted in Danish (sometimes with English translation available).
- **Forældrebestyrelse (parent board)**: elected parent representatives who advise on the institution's policies. Consider joining — it's a great way to integrate and understand the system.
- **Udviklersamtaler (development conversations)**: individual meetings with your child's kontaktpædagog to discuss your child's development, wellbeing, and progress. Held 1–2 times per year.
- **Social events**: summer parties, Christmas celebrations, workdays (where parents help maintain the playground/garden). Attending these is important for integration.
- **Daily updates**: via the **AULA app** or a physical noticeboard. Staff may also give brief verbal updates at pick-up.

> **Tip for Indian parents**: Danish daycare culture is less formal than Indian parent-teacher dynamics. Staff go by their first names. Communication is direct and honest — if your child had a difficult day, they will tell you plainly. This is not criticism; it's Danish directness.

### Transitioning Between Daycare Stages

As your child grows, they will transition between types of care:

| Transition | When | What Happens |
|---|---|---|
| Dagpleje → Vuggestue | Around 1–2 years (if applicable) | Some families switch from childminder to nursery for more socialisation |
| Vuggestue → Børnehave | Around age 3 | Most common transition; a short settling-in period (1–2 weeks) at the new institution |
| Børnehave → Børnehaveklasse | Age 5–6 | Transition to the mandatory pre-school year at the local folkeskole |
| Børnehaveklasse → School (1st class) | Age 6–7 | Start of formal schooling |

- If your child is in an **integrated institution**, the vuggestue-to-børnehave transition happens internally with minimal disruption
- Your municipality will contact you about **school enrolment** when your child approaches school age — this is separate from the daycare system

### Practical Tips for Indian Families

1. **Register immediately** — do not wait. Register on the municipal waiting list the day you get your child's CPR number. If your child is not yet born, register on the expected due date.
2. **Accept the first offer** — even if it's not your top-choice institution. You can remain on the waiting list for your preferred place while your child attends elsewhere. Declining all offers resets your waiting time.
3. **Invest in proper outdoor gear** — this is non-negotiable. Danish children play outside in all weather. Cheap rain gear and winter suits will make your child miserable. Budget DKK 1,500–3,000 for a full set of quality outdoor gear.
4. **Learn the AULA app** — this is your primary communication channel with the institution. Set it up as soon as your child starts.
5. **Don't compare to Indian daycare** — the pedagogical approach is fundamentally different. Embrace the Danish model of play-based learning.
6. **Build relationships with other parents** — offer to host playdates, attend social events, join the parent board. Your child's daycare community often becomes your family's main social network in Denmark.
7. **Ask questions** — staff are happy to explain routines, policies, and Danish customs. No question is too basic.
8. **Speak your home language at home** — your child will learn Danish at daycare. Your job is to maintain your home language. Both languages will thrive.

## Schools in Denmark

Denmark has two main options for school-age children: **Danish public schools** and **international/private schools**.

### The Danish Public School System (Folkeskole)

The **folkeskole** is Denmark's free public school system, covering ages **6–16** (Year 0 to Year 9, with an optional Year 10).

**Key features:**
- Completely **free** — no tuition fees
- Instruction is entirely in **Danish**
- Small class sizes (typically 20–28 students)
- Strong focus on group work, critical thinking, and wellbeing — not just academic performance
- No homework until older years — Denmark's educational philosophy prioritises play and balance
- **SFO** (after-school care) available at most schools until ~5 PM

**For Indian families**, the main consideration is **language**. Children who arrive speaking no Danish are given **reception classes (modtagelsesklasse)** — special integration classes that run for 1–2 years before transitioning into mainstream Danish classes. Most children pick up Danish surprisingly quickly, especially those under 10.

> **Reality check**: Children from India adapt well to the Danish school system. The environment is less pressured than Indian schooling — no rote memorisation, no board exams. Parents often find this a positive change; some find it unsettling. Both reactions are normal.

If you prefer English-medium education — or if your stay in Denmark may be temporary — international schools are the main alternative.

### Employer-Sponsored School Fees

Many multinational employers in Denmark — especially those hiring expats — will **partially or fully cover international school fees** as part of the relocation package. This is worth negotiating explicitly in your contract, as school fees can be a significant expense.

### Aarhus

- **International School Aarhus (ISAK)** — IB curriculum, well-regarded, main option for the second city

### Odense & Smaller Cities

International school options are limited outside Copenhagen and Aarhus. Families in smaller cities generally use the Danish folkeskole (with modtagelsesklasse support) or commute to Aarhus for school.

## After-School Activities for Children

Denmark has an excellent network of children's activities, all heavily subsidised:

| Activity | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Music school (musikskole)** | Municipal music schools offer instrument lessons from age 6. Very affordable. |
| **Sports clubs (idrætsforeninger)** | Football, swimming, gymnastics, martial arts — all community-run, cheap |
| **Gymnastics (gymnastik)** | Enormous in Denmark. Most towns have a community gymnastics club. |
| **Scouting (spejder)** | Danish scouting is very active, outdoor-focused, and social |
| **Art and creative workshops** | Many kulturhuse and libraries run children's workshops |
| **Swimming lessons** | Mandatory in the folkeskole, but many families also do private lessons |

## Housing Considerations for Families

### Space Requirements

Danish apartments are generally smaller than what Indian families with children might expect. When looking for family housing:

- A **3-bedroom apartment** (75–100 m²) is the minimum comfortable size for a family of four
- Most family apartments in Copenhagen are in suburbs or surrounding municipalities — **Frederiksberg, Gentofte, Lyngby, Gladsaxe, Ishøj** are popular with families
- **Detached or semi-detached houses** are common in suburban and provincial areas
- Look for proximity to schools, childcare, and public transport

### Family-Friendly Areas in Copenhagen

| Area | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Frederiksberg** | Dense, urban, excellent schools, very popular with expats |
| **Gentofte / Hellerup** | Affluent northern suburbs, CIS and Rygaards schools nearby |
| **Lyngby-Taarbæk** | More suburban, good schools, DTU nearby |
| **Valby** | More affordable, up-and-coming, good transport links |
| **Vanløse** | Family-friendly, parks, calmer than the city centre |
| **Ishøj / Brøndby** | More affordable, large Indian and South Asian community |

## Financial Benefits for Families

Denmark provides substantial financial support for families with children.

### Børne- og Ungeydelse (Child Benefit)

**All children resident in Denmark under age 18** are entitled to child benefit from the Danish state — regardless of nationality. This is paid automatically once your child has a CPR number.

**Rates (2026 approx., per child per quarter):**
| Age | Quarterly Amount (DKK) |
|---|---|
| 0 – 2 years | ~5,500 |
| 3 – 6 years | ~4,400 |
| 7 – 14 years | ~3,450 |
| 15 – 17 years | ~2,900 |

> These are paid automatically by **Udbetaling Danmark** once your child is registered. No application is usually needed.

### Maternity & Paternity Leave

Denmark has one of the world's most generous parental leave systems.

- **Total leave**: up to **52 weeks** per couple
- **Mother**: minimum 4 weeks before birth + 14 weeks after (barsel)
- **Father/co-parent**: 2 weeks immediately after birth (fædreorlov), plus additional shared weeks
- **Pay**: up to DKK ~4,460/week from the state (dagpenge), plus most employers top this up to full salary — check your contract
- Since 2022 reforms, **24 weeks are now earmarked per parent** (to encourage equal sharing), with 9 flexible weeks

> If you are on a work permit, you are entitled to parental leave if you have been working and paying into the system. Confirm your specific entitlement with your employer and HR.
