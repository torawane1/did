# India & Denmark: A History

Denmark and India share a relationship that spans over four centuries — stretching from colonial-era trading posts on the Coromandel Coast to a modern strategic partnership built on clean energy, pharmaceuticals, and digital cooperation. This is the story of that connection.

---

## The Danish East India Company & Colonial India

### The Danish East India Company (1616)

Denmark established its **Ostindisk Kompagni** (Danish East India Company) in **1616**, making it one of the earliest European powers to formally enter the Indian Ocean trade. It was founded under King Christian IV and modelled after the Dutch and English East India Companies.

The company's initial mandate was to establish trade routes for spices, textiles, and other luxury goods from Asia — competing directly with the Dutch and Portuguese who had already established dominance in the region.

### Tranquebar (Tharangambadi): Denmark's Indian Colony

The crown jewel of Danish presence in India was **Tranquebar** (Tamil: **Tharangambadi**, meaning *"town of the singing waves"*), a coastal settlement on the **Coromandel Coast** of present-day **Tamil Nadu**.

**Key dates:**
| Year | Event |
|---|---|
| **1620** | Danish Admiral **Ove Gjedde** arrives at Tranquebar and signs a treaty with the local Thanjavur Nayak ruler, **Ragunatha Nayak** |
| **1620** | Tranquebar formally becomes a Danish colony — **Dansborg Fort** is constructed |
| **1755** | Danes expand their Indian holdings, controlling Serampore (Bengal) as well |
| **1775** | Danish India reaches its greatest extent |
| **1845** | Denmark sells all its Indian territories to the British East India Company for £125,000 |

### Dansborg Fort

**Dansborg** (Danish: *Danish Castle*) was the administrative and military heart of Danish India. Built in 1620, it is one of the oldest European fortifications in India still standing today. Located directly on the beach at Tranquebar, it housed:
- The Danish colonial administration
- A garrison of soldiers and sailors
- Trading warehouses (godowns)
- A church

Today, **Dansborg Fort is a museum** maintained by the Tamil Nadu government and is a popular heritage tourist site. Much of its architecture, including the original gatehouse and ramparts, survives.

### What Denmark Traded in India

The Danish East India Company's trade at Tranquebar centred on:
- **Cotton and silk textiles** (both woven locally and obtained from Mughal-era trade networks)
- **Indigo** — highly prized in Europe as a blue dye
- **Pepper and spices**
- **Saltpetre** (potassium nitrate, essential for gunpowder)
- **Cowrie shells** (used as currency in West African slave trade routes)
- **Porcelain** (acquired via onward trade with China)

### Serampore (Frederiksnagore) — Danish Bengal

In **1755**, Denmark acquired **Serampore** (Danish: *Frederiksnagore*) on the Hooghly River in present-day **West Bengal**, just north of Calcutta. This became Denmark's second major Indian settlement.

Serampore is historically significant for a different reason than trade: it became the base of the **Serampore Mission**, founded by **William Carey** and the **Serampore Trio** (William Carey, Joshua Marshman, William Ward) — British Baptist missionaries who operated under Danish protection (as the British East India Company initially banned missionary activity in its own territories).

**The Serampore Mission's legacy:**
- Founded **Serampore College** in **1818** — one of the oldest degree-granting institutions in Asia, still functioning today
- Translated the Bible into **over 40 Indian languages and dialects**, including Bengali, Sanskrit, Marathi, Hindi, and Oriya — a monumental undertaking
- Established the first **Bengali-language printing press**
- Published early **Bengali and Sanskrit grammars**
- The college's charter was granted by the Danish Crown, giving it university status

> Serampore College's original charter (1827) from the Danish King Frederick VI granted it the right to award degrees — making it Asia's first degree-granting institution. This charter is still recognised today.

## The Nicobar Islands: Denmark's Other Indian Ocean Territory

Alongside mainland India, Denmark also controlled the **Nicobar Islands** in the Indian Ocean from **1756 to 1868**.

- Denmark claimed the islands in 1756, initially as a waystation for trade routes between India and China
- Several colonisation attempts were made but failed due to disease, hostile conditions, and logistical difficulties
- The islands were transferred to **British India in 1868**, and today form part of India's **Andaman and Nicobar Islands** union territory
- A small Danish administrative presence and place names survive in local history

## Danish Missionaries and the Tranquebar Mission

One of the most enduring legacies of Danish involvement in India is not commercial but spiritual and scholarly.

### The Tranquebar Mission (1706)

In **1706**, King Frederick IV of Denmark sponsored the **Tranquebar Mission**, sending two German Lutheran missionaries — **Bartholomäus Ziegenbalg** and **Heinrich Plütschau** — to Tranquebar. This was the **first Protestant missionary enterprise in India** and predated the British missions by nearly a century.

**Ziegenbalg's extraordinary contributions:**

| Achievement | Details |
|---|---|
| **Tamil Bible** | Translated the New Testament into Tamil by **1714** — the first translation of any part of the Bible into an Indian language |
| **Tamil grammar** | Wrote the first systematic Tamil grammar for European audiences |
| **Tamil dictionary** | Compiled a comprehensive Tamil–Portuguese–German dictionary |
| **Tamil printing press** | Brought the first printing press to India, used to print Tamil-language texts |
| **Schools** | Established schools for local children, including girls — radical for the era |
| **Malabar Hinduism** | Wrote *"Malabarisches Heidenthum"* (1711) — one of the earliest serious European academic studies of Hinduism |

Ziegenbalg died in Tranquebar in **1719**, aged just 36, but his work profoundly shaped Tamil scholarship and Indian Christian history. The **Ziegenbalg Memorial Church** in Tranquebar still stands.

### The Legacy of the Tranquebar Mission

The Tranquebar Mission created the institutional foundations for:
- Tamil-language Christian literature
- Protestant education in South India
- Early European Indology (the academic study of India)
- The Tamil Evangelical Lutheran Church, which still has millions of members today

## The Sale of Danish India (1845)

By the early 19th century, maintaining distant colonial possessions had become economically unviable for a small nation like Denmark. Following the **Napoleonic Wars** (in which Denmark backed France and suffered economically), the Danish state was in financial difficulty.

In **1845**, Denmark sold all its Indian territories — Tranquebar, Serampore, and associated settlements — to the **British East India Company** for **£125,000** (approximately DKK 1.25 million at the time).

The handover was orderly. The Danish flag was lowered at Dansborg Fort in November 1845, ending 225 years of Danish presence in India.

> The sale price was considered modest even at the time — reflecting Denmark's diminished imperial ambitions and Britain's overwhelming dominance of the subcontinent.

## Modern India–Denmark Relations

### Diplomatic History

| Year | Milestone |
|---|---|
| **1947** | India gains independence; Denmark recognises the new nation almost immediately |
| **1949** | Formal diplomatic relations established between India and Denmark |
| **1960s–80s** | Steady but modest bilateral relationship, primarily trade |
| **2009** | Enhanced partnership in renewable energy and environment |
| **2020** | **Green Strategic Partnership** declared — a landmark agreement in sustainable development cooperation |
| **2021** | Prime Minister Narendra Modi and Danish PM Mette Frederiksen hold a virtual summit |
| **2022** | Modi visits Copenhagen — first Indian PM visit to Denmark in many decades |

### The Green Strategic Partnership (2020)

The **India–Denmark Green Strategic Partnership**, signed in September 2020, is the centrepiece of modern bilateral relations. It covers:

- **Offshore wind energy** — Denmark is the world leader in offshore wind technology; India has ambitious targets (30 GW offshore by 2030)
- **Green hydrogen** — joint research and development
- **Water management** — Danish expertise in water treatment applied to India's water challenges
- **Urban planning and smart cities** — collaboration on Indian Smart Cities Mission
- **Shipping decarbonisation** — significant given that Maersk (Danish) is the world's largest container shipping company

**Denmark's Vestas** — the world's largest wind turbine manufacturer — has been present in India for decades, with major installations across Rajasthan, Tamil Nadu, and Gujarat.

### Trade and Economic Ties

**Bilateral trade (2023–24 approx.):**
- Total bilateral trade: approximately **USD 1.5–2 billion/year**
- India's exports to Denmark: pharmaceuticals, textiles, IT services, engineering goods, chemicals
- Denmark's exports to India: wind turbines, machinery, medical equipment, food processing technology, shipping equipment

**Major Danish companies in India:**

| **Maersk** | Shipping & logistics | Major port operations, logistics hubs |

| **Grundfos** | Pumps & water | Water management solutions, offices in India |
| **Danfoss** | Engineering | Refrigeration, drives, heating systems |
| **ISS** | Facilities management | Large operations across Indian corporates |

**Major Indian companies in Denmark:**

India is one of the largest sources of skilled immigrant workers in Denmark, particularly in the **IT, engineering, pharma, and academic** sectors.

### Political and Strategic Relations

- Denmark supports India's candidacy for a **permanent seat on the UN Security Council**
- Both countries cooperate in **climate negotiations** (both are committed to the Paris Agreement)
- India and Denmark collaborate within the **International Solar Alliance**
- Cultural exchanges occur through the **Danish Cultural Institute** and various academic partnerships
- The **Indian Embassy** in Copenhagen and **Danish Embassy** in New Delhi maintain active bilateral programmes

## Cultural Connections

### Indian Community in Denmark

The Indian community in Denmark today numbers approximately **15,000–20,000**, concentrated in Copenhagen, Aarhus, and Odense. This community spans:
- IT and tech professionals (a wave that began in the 2000s)
- Students at Danish universities
- Researchers and academics
- Entrepreneurs and business owners
- Long-term residents and citizens

The community has grown significantly since Denmark's **Pay Limit Scheme** and **Positive List** made it easier for skilled Indians to obtain work permits.

### Danish Interest in Indian Culture

- **Yoga** is enormously popular in Denmark — studios in every major city
- **Indian food** has a dedicated following — Copenhagen has dozens of Indian restaurants and Indian grocery stores
- **Bollywood** has a niche but enthusiastic following
- Danish universities offer **Indology, South Asian Studies**, and **Sanskrit** courses
- The **Danish Cultural Institute** has historically fostered connections between Denmark and South Asia

### Tranquebar Today: A Living Heritage Site

Tranquebar (Tharangambadi) remains one of the most tangible links between India and Denmark. Today it is a small coastal town in Tamil Nadu's Nagapattinam district, notable for:

- **Dansborg Fort** — now a museum, free to visit
- **New Jerusalem Church (1718)** — built by the Danish missionaries, still in use
- **Ziegenbalg's House** — the original home of the missionary, preserved
- **The King's House** — former Danish administrative building
- **Danish-era bungalows** — colonial architecture scattered through the town
- Annual **Tranquebar Festival** celebrating the Indo-Danish heritage

The **Museum Tranquebar project** — a collaboration between the National Museum of Denmark and the Tamil Nadu government — has worked to document and preserve this shared heritage.

> **Visiting Tranquebar**: It is approximately 280 km south of Chennai (Madras). The town is small, unhurried, and genuinely atmospheric — a rare surviving slice of Danish colonial India.
