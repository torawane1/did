# First 30 Days

The first month in Denmark is a bureaucratic sprint. Each item below unlocks the next — the order matters. This page gives you the correct sequence and what to expect at each step.

---

## The Dependency Chain

Understanding what unlocks what saves enormous frustration:

```
Address registration → CPR number → MitID → Bank account → Everything else
```

You **cannot** open a bank account without MitID (in most cases).
You **cannot** get MitID without a CPR number.
You **cannot** get a CPR number without a registered address.

Start with housing — even temporary — before anything else.

---

## Step 1: Register Your Address

**Do this: Within the first week of arriving.**

You must register your address at the local **Borgerservice** (Citizens' Service centre) in your municipality. Copenhagen's main Borgerservice is at:

> Borgerservice, Dahlerupsgade 6, 1640 Copenhagen V

**What to bring:**
- Passport
- Residence permit (the physical card or the letter from SIRI)
- Proof of your address (rental contract, or a letter from your employer / host if staying temporarily)
- Employment contract

**What you get:**
- Address registered in the National Register (CPR Register)
- Your CPR number is typically issued **within a few days** (sometimes same day)

> If you're in a temporary flat or Airbnb at first, you can still register — bring a letter from the landlord confirming your stay. Update your address when you find permanent housing.

**Municipality websites:**
- Copenhagen: [kk.dk/borgerservice](https://www.kk.dk)
- Aarhus: [aarhus.dk](https://www.aarhus.dk)
- Odense: [odense.dk](https://www.odense.dk)

## Step 2: CPR Number

**CPR** (Central Person Register) is your Danish social security and tax identification number. It's a 10-digit number in the format **DDMMYY-XXXX** (date of birth + 4 digits).

**Why it matters:** Almost every interaction with Danish society — healthcare, tax, banking, subscriptions — requires your CPR number. Getting this right is the highest priority.

**How you get it:**
- Automatically issued when you register your address (Step 1)
- You'll receive a letter in the post with your CPR number, usually within 1–5 working days
- Some Borgerservice offices issue it on the spot

**Once you have your CPR number:**
- Register with a GP (doctor) — see [Daily Life](#daily-life)
- Proceed to get MitID (Step 3)

---

## Step 3: MitID

**MitID** is Denmark's national digital identity system — it's like Aadhaar + banking 2FA combined. You use it to:
- Log in to government portals (SKAT, e-Boks, Borger.dk)
- Verify your identity for your bank
- Sign documents digitally
- Access your payslips and tax information

**How to get MitID:**
1. Visit [mitid.dk](https://www.mitid.dk) and click "Get MitID"
2. You'll need your CPR number to start
3. **In-person activation**: Go to a Borgerservice office (same place you registered your address) with your passport. This is the most reliable method.
4. Alternatively, some banks can activate MitID during account opening
5. You'll set up the **MitID app** on your phone — this becomes your main authentication method

> **Note**: MitID replaced NemID in 2023. If anyone mentions NemID, they're referring to the old system.

## Step 4: Bank Account

### Which Bank?

For most new arrivals, the easiest options are:

| Bank | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Lunar** | 100% digital, English app, relatively easy to open with CPR + passport |
| **Danske Bank** | Largest bank, has English support, requires in-person branch visit |
| **Nordea** | Good for international transfers, English support |
| **Nykredit** | Competitive for mortgages later |

**For new arrivals, Lunar is often the easiest entry point** — they have an English-language app, no branch visit required for basic account, and decent FX rates.

### Opening an Account

**You'll need:**
- CPR number
- Passport / residence permit
- Danish address (proof)
- MitID (Lunar and some others require it to activate fully)

**Timeline:** Lunar can be done in a day or two. Traditional banks often require a branch appointment and may take 1–2 weeks.

### Practical Banking Tips

- Get a **MasterCard debit** (Visa Dankort or international debit/credit) — most Danish shops still prefer Dankort (domestic Visa) for smallest purchases
- Set up **Nets (MobilePay)** — this is the dominant mobile payment app in Denmark, used peer-to-peer (like UPI) and at many stores and markets. You cannot function socially without it.
- Enable international transfers from day one — you'll want to send money to India

## Step 5: Tax Card (Skattekort)

Your employer needs your **tax card** from SKAT before they can process your first payslip. This usually happens automatically once your CPR is registered, but it's worth confirming.

- Log in to [skat.dk](https://www.skat.dk) with MitID
- Check **Forskudsopgørelse** (your preliminary tax assessment) — make sure the income figure matches your actual salary
- Your employer will use your tax card rate to deduct the correct tax from your pay

See [Money & Tax](#money-and-tax) for a full breakdown of the Danish tax system.

## Step 6: SIM Card

Getting a Danish number makes everything easier — WhatsApp with colleagues, delivery drivers calling, government letters via SMS.

### Recommended Options

| Provider | Best for | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Lebara** | Most popular among Indian expats | Low-cost, good EU roaming, easy registration |
| **CBB Mobil** | Value plans | Reliable, Danish network (uses TDC/Nuuday) |
| **Oister** | Mid-range | English app, good data plans |
| **Telenor** | International calls | Good for calling India |

**To register a SIM**, you'll need your CPR number and passport. Most SIMs are registered via the provider's website.

### Calling India

- Lebara often has the best rates for international calls to India (BSNL, Airtel, Jio)
- WhatsApp and Google Voice are your best friends for free calls
- Consider keeping an eSIM with an Indian number active for OTPs from Indian banks

## Step 7: Housing

Finding long-term housing in Copenhagen and other Danish cities is competitive. Here's what you need to know.

### The Market Reality

- Copenhagen rental market is **tight** — vacancy rates are low and good flats go fast
- Expect to pay **DKK 8,000–15,000/month** for a 1-bedroom in Copenhagen
- Aarhus and Odense are more affordable (DKK 6,000–10,000/month for 1-bed)
- Many listings require a CPR number — another reason to sort that first

### Where to Search

| Platform | Notes |
|---|---|
| **[BoligPortal.dk](https://www.boligportal.dk)** | Largest private rental listings site |
| **[Lejebolig.dk](https://www.lejebolig.dk)** | Also large, subscribe for email alerts |
| **[Facebook groups](https://www.facebook.com)** | "Rooms/Flats for rent in Copenhagen" groups — surprisingly active |
| **[Findroommate.dk](https://www.findroommate.dk)** | For finding flatmates |
| **Your employer** | Ask HR — many larger companies have partnerships with corporate housing |

### Lease Basics

- Standard leases are 1-year minimum, with 3-month notice period
- Landlords typically ask for **3 months deposit + 3 months prepaid rent** upfront
- Tenant rights are strong in Denmark — familiarise yourself with [Lejeloven](https://www.retsinformation.dk) (Rent Act)
- Your employer will likely cover a portion of your initial relocation housing costs — check your contract

### Temporary Housing

While you hunt for a flat:
- **Corporate housing / serviced apartments** — ask your company's HR
- **Airbnb** — expensive but flexible
- **Kollegier (student housing)** — sometimes available to non-students short-term
- Fellow Indians in the community often know of short-term room rentals — check the community Facebook groups in [Daily Life](#daily-life)

## 30-Day Checklist

Use this as your master checklist:

- [ ] **Day 1–3**: Register address at Borgerservice
- [ ] **Day 3–7**: Receive CPR number
- [ ] **Day 5–7**: Get MitID at Borgerservice
- [ ] **Day 7–14**: Open bank account (start with Lunar while waiting for traditional bank)
- [ ] **Day 1**: Get a local SIM card (bring your passport)
- [ ] **Day 7–14**: Register with a GP (doctor) — requires CPR
- [ ] **Day 7–21**: Log in to SKAT.dk and verify tax card / Forskudsopgørelse
- [ ] **Day 7–21**: Set up MobilePay
- [ ] **Ongoing**: Hunt for permanent housing
- [ ] **Week 3–4**: Set up e-Boks (your official digital mailbox for all government letters)
- [ ] **Week 4**: Confirm payroll setup with employer (CPR + bank account details)

---

## e-Boks: Your Digital Mailbox

**e-Boks** is the mandatory digital letter system used by the Danish government and most banks. All official letters — tax documents, permit renewals, healthcare correspondence — arrive here.

- Sign up at [eboks.dk](https://www.e-boks.com) using your CPR number and MitID
- Set up email notifications so you don't miss anything
- This replaces physical post for almost all official correspondence
